TechCrunch AI

Anthropic's Fable 5 So Powerful the White House Forced It Offline

Anthropic launched Fable 5 and its companion cybersecurity model Mythos 5 as the most capable AI systems the company has ever built. Within days, Amazon researchers demonstrated a jailbreak that stripped the model's safety filters, reportedly exposing advanced capabilities in areas sensitive enough to trigger White House intervention on national security grounds. The administration forced Anthropic to pull both models from distribution. It also revoked access for SK Telecom over alleged ties to China. Cybersecurity researchers responded with an open letter calling the move dangerous, noting that identical jailbreak techniques work on competing models from OpenAI and Google. Anthropic itself pointed out the same flaw. What makes this story historic is not the vulnerability. It is that a frontier model demonstrated such a leap in raw power that the executive branch of the US government acted in real time to contain it.

For RAMSAC's clients, this story lands on two levels. First, it confirms that AI capability is accelerating faster than anyone predicted. Models are now powerful enough to warrant government-level responses within days of release. Second, it underscores why cybersecurity strategy must evolve. If a model can be jailbroken to bypass its own guardrails, then every business using AI needs layered defences that do not rely on the model policing itself. This is exactly the kind of conversation an RM can open with a client: your AI tools are only as safe as the security architecture around them. RAMSAC's managed security services become more valuable every time a story like this breaks. Watch for Anthropic to re-release these models with additional safeguards, and expect every competitor to face the same scrutiny.

The Decoder

Sakana AI's Fugu Orchestrates Multiple LLMs Into a Single System That Rivals the Best

Sakana AI, a Japanese research lab, has released Fugu, a system built on multi-model architectures that dynamically routes tasks to whichever AI model handles them best. Rather than relying on a single massive model, Fugu orchestrates several smaller models working together in a form of multi-agent interaction. The result matches the performance of Anthropic's Fable 5 and Mythos on key benchmarks while dramatically reducing dependence on any one provider. Think of it as a conductor leading an orchestra where each instrument is a different AI model, and the conductor decides in real time which one plays each note. The approach also reduces costs because smaller specialised models are cheaper to run than one enormous general-purpose system.

This matters for RAMSAC clients immediately. Vendor lock-in is one of the biggest concerns SMEs have about adopting AI. If you build everything on one provider and they raise prices, get banned, or suffer an outage, your business stops. Fugu points toward a future where IT strategy includes model diversification, the same way businesses diversify cloud providers today. For RMs, the talking point is clear: your AI strategy should not bet everything on one model or one vendor. RAMSAC can help clients design architectures that are resilient, cost-effective, and future-proof. This is a strategic IT consulting conversation waiting to happen.

The Decoder

OpenAI's Codex Watches You Work Once and Repeats the Task Forever

OpenAI has released Record and Replay for its Codex application on macOS. The concept is deceptively simple: a user performs a task on their computer exactly once while Codex watches. The system converts that demonstration into a reusable "skill" that it can then execute on its own, indefinitely, without further human input. This is not traditional automation where a developer writes scripts. This is an AI agent that learns by observation. Show it how you format a weekly report, how you move data between applications, or how you process invoices, and it builds an autonomous workflow from what it saw. The feature is not yet available in the UK, EU, or Switzerland due to regulatory considerations, but its arrival is expected.

When this lands in the UK, it will transform how SMEs think about operational efficiency. Every business has repetitive digital tasks that consume hours of skilled workers' time. Record and Replay means those tasks become one-time demonstrations rather than ongoing labour. For RAMSAC, this opens two conversations. First, managed IT services will need to support, secure, and govern these autonomous skills running across client environments. Who controls what Codex can access? How do you audit what it does? Second, IT strategy consulting can help clients identify which workflows to automate first and how to do it safely. The RM talking point: when this arrives, you will want a partner who can help you deploy it securely and get the most value from it immediately.

Samsung Deploys ChatGPT Enterprise and Codex to All Employees

Samsung Electronics has deployed ChatGPT Enterprise and OpenAI's Codex platform to all employees in South Korea plus everyone in its global Device eXperience division. This is remarkable context: in 2023, Samsung famously banned ChatGPT after employees accidentally leaked proprietary source code through the tool. Three years later, the same company is making it standard infrastructure for its entire workforce. The shift happened because enterprise-grade AI tools now offer data isolation, audit trails, and administrative controls that did not exist when Samsung first encountered the risk. ChatGPT Enterprise keeps company data separate from OpenAI's training pipeline and gives IT administrators full visibility into usage.

Ars Technica AI

Critical Copilot Vulnerability Let Hackers Steal 2FA Codes from Users

Security researchers disclosed a critical vulnerability in Microsoft Copilot that exploited the SearchLeak technique to extract sensitive data, including two-factor authentication codes, from users. The attack worked by manipulating how Copilot retrieves and presents information from connected data sources. Because Copilot has access to emails, files, and messages across Microsoft 365, a successful exploit could pull authentication codes sent via email before the user even saw them. Microsoft has patched the vulnerability, but the disclosure underscores a structural challenge: the more access you give an AI tool, the more damage a successful attack can do. Ars Technica noted this as evidence that the industry's approach to LLM security continues to fail in predictable ways.

For RAMSAC clients already using or considering Microsoft 365 Copilot, this is not a reason to avoid the tool. It is a reason to deploy it with expert security management. The vulnerability was patched quickly, but only organisations with proper monitoring and update management would have been protected in the window before the fix. This is exactly why managed IT and cybersecurity services exist. An RM should frame this as reassurance, not alarm: yes, AI tools can be targeted, just like any powerful business software. The difference is whether you have a security partner watching for these threats and applying patches the moment they land. RAMSAC clients with managed security were protected. Companies managing their own IT may not have been.
